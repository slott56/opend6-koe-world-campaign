Introduction¶

The Kingdom of the East is in chaotic, bloody turmoil.

The regime created by the Eighth Allocation of lands is tottering toward collapse. The current king, from Hillshire, has done two things to undermine his own reign. First, he’s sown distrust among the earls by encouraging squabbles and failing to resolve minor border disputes. Second, he’s failed to put stop to piracy along the north coast. This only compounds the internal distrust.

The king retains the crown for one reason. This is the same reason all kings of this kingdom hold onto power. The Council of Earls is too fractious to prop up one Earl who will be recognized as King by the other earls. As they argue among themselves, King Hillshire remains in power.

The ongoing border disputes within the kingdom create the kind of chaos where a group of well-organized bandits can do real damage. They could usurp an earl who’s too focused on events in distant parts of the kingdom to monitor the roads under his own nose. They could pit two earls against each other.

Historically, the allocation of lands among the earls is how power was balanced among the Earls. Some changes within the Temple of Cyþan – where mages are trained – had upset the power balance. It’s still in the process of settling down.

For more, see OpenD6 Fantasy Worldbook: Kingdom of the East.

In this chapter, we’ll look at the overall background for the campaign: the Kingdom of the East. The structure of the Kingdom is defined the Land Allocations, creating endless opportunities for war and intrigue. We’ll also look at magic in this world, which has some unique features.

Closer to the characters’ home in Hillshire, their earl seems to be stumbling toward war with the Earl Southlands. We’ll cover this, as well as the complications arising from additional powers creating havoc.

We’ll start with an overview of the kingdom.

Allocations¶

The kingdom is composed of six Earls – Northlands, Westmarch, Hillshire, Southlands, South Coast, Eastmarch – and their King. The king id formerly from Hillshire, and retains that name. (It’s odd, but it’s never ambiguous: the two people are called “King Hillshire” and “Earl Hillshire.”) The king has a small bit of land – the Royal Domain – carved out of the three wealthiest earldoms: Northlands, Westmarch, and Eastmarch. This gives the king a modicum of personal power. Practically, power comes from organizing the earls, and the knights they command.

Beyond the western border, there are some Outland Throngs perhaps aligned with Westmarch or Hillshire. They’re sometimes helpful in protecting the border, but they don’t represent a significant economic presence.

A king starts out as an Earl until the other earls recognize them individual to serve as a supreme leader in defense of the kingdom. While the role has important symbolic value, any action by the king requires consent of other one or more earls to provide the people, horses, and equipment. When an Earl is elevated to King, they must pass control of their (former) earldom to a successor. While the successor is often an adult child, it is equally likely to be a spouse, a trusted knight, or even one of the Great Priests of one of the temples. The idea of dynastic succession isn’t popular in the Kingdom of the East.

One of the things the king can do is to balance power among the earls. Where necessary, there can be a reallocation of the borders of the earldoms. Ideally, this happens as the kingdom expands.

During the Fifth Allocation, for example, a new Earldom, Hillshire, was added. The newly-installed earl had control over land extracted from Southlands and Westmarch, as well as land formerly considered to be Outlands, beyond the kingdom’s borders.

The important principle is this: Kings come and go. According to some historians, a king shouldn’t require more than a decade to defeat enemies and set things right. This puts pressure on kings to understand their mandate and solve the problem they were handed.

The Allocations of land, on the other hand, are rare; in the last thousand years, eight have been recorded. The allocations serve as important historical guideposts, dividing the time into regimes based on the structure of the kingdom. Some regimes have had as many as a dozen kings. One only had two in succession before a reallocation was needed to restore peace.

Each Earl has control over several large temples reflecting the six religious orders prevent in the Kingdom of the East. The Temples of Cyþan and Hildeþrymm. THe Cyþan temples are here the great mage forges were located. The Hildeþrymm temples train the fighters who’ll become squires and knights, central to an Earl’s power. The followers of Hildeþrymm will travel to where they were needed. Traditionally, the mages were confined to the forges located in their temples. This meant the mages would need to use artifacts and sympathetic bindings to project power to where it was needed.

Less than one hundred years ago, the relationship with the Temple of Cyþan started to change.

Mages and Forges¶

The Seventh allocation’s collapse coincided with the decline of the huge fixed forges, central to the Temples of Cyþan. These immense structures have been partially displaced by portable forges. There are a number of conflicting stories of how this change began. It may have been a mage from Eastmarch who had uncovered a new approach to managing the elemental forces of the universe. It may have been a wizard from the Western empire who revealed the secret to mages working for Earl Westmarch. It may have been Outlander shamans with techniques for harnessing elemental powers that were adapted by mages in Hillshire.

The portable forges changed the face of warfare, permitting mages to move more freely. This eliminated the logistic complexities of coordinating activities of mages on the battlefront with those back in the temple. It also created a new complexity of getting a mage and a portable forge to the battlefront. (Not everyone learns at the same pace, the Earl of South Cost notably dismissed portable forges as “weak” and “too complicated”. To this day, South Coast remains the poorest of the earldoms.)

Using a forge for an enchantment

Fixed Forges.

For very powerful, very complex operations, a giant, fixed forge is very helpful. It requires a ring of 5 helpers, who can add dice to the mage’s enchantment. There are two results:

  • Based on the Community aspect of an enchantment, if all 6 mages in the ring make their difficulty roll for the chant (difficulty 11), the difficulty for the overall enchantment is adjusted by -4.

  • The ready availability of the elements, means a forge creates a 2× increase in the effect.

Portable Forges.

For portable power, a mobile forge is very helpful. Helpers are not required.

  • Based on the Component aspect of an enchantment, a portable forge, with a bucket of water, and a dish of clean, dry sand will provide all of the elements. There may be difficulty increases for elements not readily available.

  • The forge’s power source creates 1.5× increase in effect.

For more details, see OpenD6 Fantasy Worldbook: Kingdom of the East, “Magic”.

Trade and Transport¶

In this world, there is no wheel. Goods (and people) move on horseback. Goods are loaded on on a drag sled (or “travois”) and dragged behind a horse. Small loads (max 30 kg) may be dragged by a dog.

GM Note

For more information on the world, see OpenD6 Fantasy Worldbook: Kingdom of the East.

Merchant caravans are how business is done over long distances. Because of the value of the goods (and the value of the horses and tackle), these tend to be heavily-armed military affairs. Well-respected caravans can also carry mail and treasure across the kingdom.

The central role of caravans means that the stables and corrals are often prominent features of small towns and villages along the trade routes. A tavern that caters to travelers will have a large front-yard for the caravan animals. A corral will be near one or more taverns and bath houses. Outfitters and caravan supply companies will be located nearby, also.

Additionally, per royal decree, there are taverns on the main trading routes. These are often small villages with a few essential services like a farrier, a smith, and a saddle-maker. The taverns are not expected to be self-supporting. The owner has a healthy line of credit, and some bills may be paid by the king. Accounting is essential, otherwise, it looks like waste and fraud. This system helps offer safety for travelers.

The Calendar

See OpenD6 Fantasy Worldbook: Kingdom of the East , “Equipment” for additional details about equipment and coinage.

The Kingdom of the East divides the year into six seasons, each lasting 60 days, and 5 or 6 extra days outside the seasonal calendar. The names are Stirring, Spring, Summer, Harvest, Fading, and Winter. The Midsummer and Midwinter solstices are on the 15th of Summer and 15th of Winter. The Equinoxes are on the first day of Spring and the last day of Harvest.

The extra days are inserted between seasons. These days don’t have a week-day, and simply aren’t counted. Most years have 5 extra days; the Temple of Cyþan has a scheme for adjusting which pair of seasons will lack a holiday between them. Every four years or so there will be an extra day in front of each season.

The scheme isn’t perfect, and adjustments are made to be sure solstices and equinoxes are on the correct days. Generally, the Temple of Cyþan and Witan both track the celestial events, and tend to agree without much bloodshed.

The View From the Hill: City of Eagles¶

The City of Eagles – at one time – guarded the western borders of the Kingdom of the East. Arriving and departing caravans made extensive use of the city’s hospitality and other services like farriers and smiths. Recent wars have expanded the kingdom: Eagles is no longer on the border. This altered the caravan routes, stripping valuable revenue from the city.

Sir Warrant, Defender of City of Eagles, a High Knight, has been studying the road from the gates of his city, looking down into the folded hills of Southlands. In the distance lies the City of White Water where the land drops suddenly to the coastal plains. The City of White Water, being small, is administered by a Common Knight, Sir Bond, Defender of City of White Water.

The City of Grace’s vast seaport is accessible by river boat from the City of White Water, where every caravan must stop or start. This gives White Water an edge on trade the Eagles can’t touch.

Sir Warrant has considered reshaping the border as one possibility of restoring City of Eagles to prominence. Sir Warrant can raise a larger army from the City of Eagles than anything Sir Bond could field from his large town of White Water. With support from the Earl of Hillshire, this kind of bold action might be possible. It involves seizing a large swath of the western edge of Southlands, something that would require a great deal of military support.

The current Earl Hillshire is not open to an outright war with Southlands, Further, there’s a Hillshire as King and the King can’t seem to focus on useful internal politics at all. Further, Hillshire may be Westmarch’s puppet.

From Sir Warrant’s perspective, all of the revenue-generating opportunities are located within Southlands. A blockade of the road would result in caravans finding a way around his City of Eagles. Forcing caravans to divert north through Westmarch would add weeks to the trip, and revenue would be shifted to Westmarch.

The remaining option available to Sir Warrant is appealing to Sir Bond of White Water’s family, and Sir Bond’s wealth. Bond has a marriageable daughter; a family tie may permit nobles from the City of Eagles to become a valuable part of the caravan route again. The actual union may never come to fruition; Sir Warrant’s son does not have a head for trade or mercantile activities, and Sir Bond’s daughter seems unlikely to divert any of the revenue from the caravan traffic.

One benefit of starting these discussions is to open some diplomatic channels which had previously been closed. Looking at the bigger picture, having Eagles and White Water working together creates ways for Hillshire and Southlands to cooperate. If Hillshire and Southlands can form some kind of alliance, they might be able to shift the center of power and have a King friendly to their alliance.

The most recent parts of this conflict are detailed in the Scenarios chapter.

The Complication¶

These delicate negotiations are complicated by the surrounding chaos and events precipitated by outsiders. Each of the knights has been blaming the other for bandits on the road from Eagles to White Water. Bandits and highwaymen, well-armed, and well-organized, are everywhere and seem to be able to flee into the hills along the border with impunity.

It’s difficult to pursue the bandits without staging an armed incursion into someone else’s earldom.

In years past, Caravans would pause for resupply in the City of Eagles. Now, some caravan captains feel that stopping in Eagles means spies will pass word to the bandits. They prefer to pass straight through.

When the City of Eagles looks like a baited trap, it helps the bandits. They can waylay caravans on down to the dregs of their supplies.

When the Sir Bond of White Water arrives in the City of Eagles, the real troubles will begin.

More Background¶

Some Geography¶

The Seventh Allocation – like the Fifth – was thrown into bloody chaos with the assassination of the king. This opened the door to a period of war and shifting alliances. The Earls fought, made and broke alliances, and hoped to find a coalition where one could name themself king and end the fighting. Through advantageous geography, Southlands extensive trade networks across the seas had the influence needed for Earl Southlands to be elevated to king

US Comparison

The Kingdom of the East is a peninsula, about 750km east to west, and close to 800km north to south. The North-South direction is about the distance from Richmond, VA, to Jacksonville, FL. In the East-West direction it covers the stance from Richmond to Nashville, TN.

Across the seas to the north, there’s a separate content. Isolated raiders from the north may be working for the king. Any of the earls on the northern border – Northlands and Eastmarch – have trouble maintaining a navy as well as defending their internal borders from the other earls. The king isn’t helping here, which is compounding the kingdom’s internal problems.

Below the salt-marshes to the south, the Kingdom of Mossing presents a minor threat. The trackless, dangerous marshes ensure a certain kind of safety from this border.

To the west, past a wide belt called the Outlands, lies a vast empire. In the Kingdom of the East, they call it the “Western Empire”. The few travelers who speak the Imperial trade language know it as the Garden Empire.

Generally, the Empire’s focus is on other lands to the west and north. The size of the Empire means the western border Earls – Westmarch, Hillshire, and Northlands – cannot defend themselves without a King.

The Earl of South Coast is rarely important in the kingdom’s affairs. South Coast is low-lying sandy land with off-shore sandbars and few useful ports. With an economy based on subsistence fishing, no earl has the money to raise a proper army and pursue the crown.

Generally, trade caravans pass from the Empire through Hillshire to the seaports in Southlands. With rampant piracy on the north coast, this has started to become the center of commerce for the kingdom.

Some History¶

The Earls of the Hillshire and Southlands were never on speaking terms.

One source of problems for Earl Hillshire is the awkward border with Southlands. There’s no natural definition; without a clear mountain ridge or a river, the border is dismayingly flexible. During the Fifth Allocation someone drew a line on a map without regard for any allegiances or existing trading relationships among the rural towns and villages.

Another source of problems is the cities of Southlands dominating the flow of trade goods. Southlands has a deep, navigable seaport surrounded by the sprawling seaport City of Grace.

From Hillshire’s perspective, a third problem is Southlands encroaching on the border lawfully decreed by the King and the council of Earls in the Fifth Allocation.

The cities of Hillshire, on the other hand, tend to be sources of materials like timber and ore. The City of Eagles is perched high on a ridge, not far from the haunted, abandoned iron mines sometimes called the Red Mound or the Red Howe. The road to the Western Empire goes through here, and heavily-armed caravans frequently fight their way through the Outlands.

From Southland’s perspective, Hillshire is a chimera, stitched together from parts torn out of Southlands and lands inhabited by Outlanders from beyond the kingdom. The Outlanders speak an archaic language and live at a paleolithic level, one can’t expect them to interact with the more cultured and educated Earls of the Kingdom of the East. (The fact that Westmarch also gave up land to create Hillshire is something actively ignored in Earl Southland’s grievance.)

Hillshire has only been part of the kingdom since the Fifth Allocation of lands, about 126 years ago. A number of Earls whisper about the 11th King of the Fifth allocation – King Hillshire – being assassinated only 76 days after accepting the crown. They claim this shows regrets for adding Hillshire to the kingdom. There’s no hint of regret in the written record; to a committed conspiracist this lack of evidence is only more evidence for a broad conspiracy of silence.

Southlands has two separate sources of revenue from Western caravans, and will not let anyone from Hillshire forget it. One group of income sources includes the seaport hostelries, taverns, public houses, and longshore cargo handlers all making money from sea-going trade. Another group includes the City of White Water carters, handlers, and caravanseries who also take substantial fees to move goods up and down the steep road around the falls. White Water is a boom-town located where the road meets the river.
