Equipment¶

This chapter gives game mechanics for popular equipment, including gear, armor, and weapons, plus how to purchase them. Generally, the OpenD6 Fantasy rules can be used with some modifications. Parts of the OpenD6 Fantasy, “Equipment” chapter can be used.

The notable exceptions are the lack of wheeled vehicles and the lack of sophisticated gunpowder weapons.

Purchasing Equipment¶

The OpenD6 Fantasy rules can be used without modification. See the OpenD6 Fantasy, PURCHASING EQUIPMENT section.

Coin equivalence

“…eight copper pieces equals one silver piece; eight silver pieces equals one gold piece…”

Funds

Any item with a price difficulty equal to or less than the number in front of the “D” in the character’s Funds attribute is an automatic purchase. A character may make several automatic purchases per day.

Any item with a price difficulty greater than the number in front of the “D” in the character’s Funds attribute but less than or equal to 3 times that number is an average purchase. A character may make one average purchase per day.

Any item with a price difficulty greater than 3 times the number in front of the “D” in the character’s Funds attribute is a luxury purchase. A character may make one luxury purchase every seven days.

Mystical Artifacts and Holy Items¶

These are powerful artifacts unique to this world.

Wands/Staves¶

Wands, rods, and staves are all very similar. Wands tend to be small (well under 1m). These are the violins and violas of magical devices. Rods tend to be larger; generally a bit over a meter, and much heavier. A rod could be used as a heavy cane or walking stick. Think of ‘cellos. Staves are quite large, often over 2 meters long. These are the large contrabass-scale magical devices.

The linear shape means they are constrained by having a limited number of charges, and must be recharged.

They are bound to a specific enchantment or invocation.

Books: Codexes and Scrolls¶

Books in both codex-form (pages bound on an edge) and scroll-form (pages stitched and rolled) can help a mage or priest perform an enchantment. The written form of an enchantment can provides up to +1D bonus to the effect (it has no impact on establishing a link.)

The temple of Cyþan requires mages of all ranks to keep a journal.

Most mages at the rank of High Mage or higher will invest time in research. This includes copying notes and journal entries from other high mages as well as summarizing their journal entries. This is a kind of scientific method with experimental changes to an enchantment and measurements of effects. At the heart of this, mages are studying how the Æther decomposes into elements, and how the things we observe are composed of elements.

Books are generally unique. Some foundational texts are copied in their entirety; a temple may have multiple copies.

Over the centuries, of course, books can be lost or stolen. More commonly, individual mage journals are lost or found by travelers.

A single-page scroll with a copy of a potion recipe or enchantment litany may be traded among mages. The prices for such things are negotiated, and depend on how desperate the parties involved are.

The Lessons of Cyþan

This book is a sacred text that contains lessons of the core teachings of Cyþan. It was originally written in an older language, and some copies of the original are rumored to exist.

There are no new enchantments in this book.

Indeed, it’s view of the system of correspondencies is archaic, and has been refined and clarified. Some mages think that it might be beneficial to revert to this classical form of enchanting. They often find it less effective, but more spiritually fulfilling.

Vehicles¶

The OpenD6 Fantasy rules are incompatible with this world, and should not be used. The wheel doesn’t exist in this world.

In this world, there is no wheel. Goods (and people) move on horseback. Goods are loaded on on a drag sled (or “travois”) and dragged behind a horse. Small loads (max 30 kg) may be dragged by a dog.

There are two styles of construction, sometimes called “A” and “H”. The “A”-shaped frame is suitable for a dog. The “H” style is generally used for a horse. Because a simple “A” frame can be built quickly, it’s sometimes used a temporary sled.

The roads tend to be the width of a horses’s “H” frame. Only the two edges – where the frame would drag – are paved. The paving is close-fitting stones without an obvious gap. The center of the road is left as bare earth and whatever weeds can survive the passing traffic. Royal roads tend to be build on beds of gravel and timber so rainwater drains away, preserving the paving stones.

Merchant caravans are how business is done over long distances. They are generally heavily armed, and may have scores of horses, each dragging a sled with supplies or goods.

The section on SHIP WEAPONS, however, can be used.

The Calendar¶

The Kingdom of the East divides the year into six seasons, each lasting 60 days, and 5 or 6 extra days outside the seasonal calendar. The names are Stirring, Spring, Summer, Harvest, Fading, and Winter. The Midsummer and Midwinter solstices are on the 15th of Summer and 15th of Winter. The Equinoxes are on the first day of Spring and the last day of Harvest.

The extra days are inserted between seasons. These days don’t have a week-day, and simply aren’t counted. Most years have 5 extra days; the Temple of Cyþan has a scheme for adjusting which pair of seasons will lack a holiday between them. Every four years or so there will be an extra day in front of each season.

The scheme isn’t perfect, and adjustments are made to be sure solstices and equinoxes are on the correct days. Generally, the Temple of Cyþan and Witan both track the celestial events, and tend to agree without much bloodshed.
