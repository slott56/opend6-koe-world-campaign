Movement¶

Getting from here to there by any means – running, swimming, flying, using a vehicle, you name it – is what this chapter’s all about. Generally, the OpenD6 Fantasy rules can be used with some modifications. The OpenD6 Fantasy, “Movement”, chapter can be used with a few significant deletions.

Perhaps the most significant is the lack of the wheel. With sufficient magic, the wheel isn’t needed. This means conventional land vehicles do not exist in this world. Indeed, the wheel (and similar technologies like screws and bolts) don’t exist anywhere except in the minds of the insane.

This means everything heavy or bulky is moved on horseback (or mule or ox.) All of the vehicle attacking and dodging is simply fighting against an animal with a mounted rider.

See Vehicles for more details.

The Sailing Ships section does apply, however, since sailing ships are common. One nuance is the use of block-and-tackle to sail a ship. Without a rotating sheave, pulleys have a lot of friction. This limits the size of sails and anchors, which – in turn – limits the size of ships. This means this world limits most ships to a length of no more than 15m, and a 100kg anchor.

To have a larger ship requires the active participation of mages to provide the needed assistance in raising anchors and raising sails. The usual approach for aiding sailors is an enchantment to transfer some of the earth element out of the anchor (or a heavy spar or sail) into the ship’s ballast. When the spell duration expires, the element returns, and the anchor recovers its weight. This also means that mages can manipulate air and water elements to move the ship when the weather won’t cooperate.

For reference

Columbus crossed the Atlantic in three 15m-long ships, each with a crew of 24. Shipping of this scale is prevalent in this world.

In 1770, James Cook sailed to New Zealand in the Endeavor. This vast ship was almost 40m in length, had masts 18m tall, and a crew of 94. A ship of this scale would requires numerous mages to manipulate heavy sails and anchors.
