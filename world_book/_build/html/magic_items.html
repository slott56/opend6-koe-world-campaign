Magical Items and Potions¶

There are few magical items described in the OpenD6 Fantasy rules. See OpenD6 Magic Guide OpenD6 Magic Guide, Magic Items and OpenD6 Magic Guide, Potions chapters for useful background on these to subjects.

See Equipment for copies of these descriptions.

In principle, any object can be enchanted. The object is a vehicle, which contains an enchantment. The object embodies the mage’s skill rolls, making it possible for non-mages to use enchantments.

Pragmatically, getting the balance of elements correct is surprisingly difficult when working with complex objects. The most complex things that are enchanted are weapons and armor. These bundles of metal, leather, and wood reflect the limit of complexity for most mages. Simple things, like rings, wands, cups, and the like, are often of a few few and very pure materials, making them relatively expensive, but also easier to work with.

There are a variety of general types of magical items, most of which have distinct associations with the six temples.

Rings¶

Rings don’t have a Charges aspect. The circular shape somehow mitigates the discharge of the enchantment, allowing rings to operate for long periods of time without being re-enchanted.

Rings are generally associated with the Temple of Witan. The priesthood’s symbols of office are rings with complex histories. Some have the power to heal or help with disabilities.

The holy symbol for Witan is a begging bowl, a kind of ring, often carved of wood, but sometimes the bottom of a broken clay pitcher.

Wands, staves, rods, batons, and cudgels¶

These must have charges. The linear shape leads to a discharge of the enchantment. The number of charges can be large, but these devices must be re-enchanted periodically.

These are associated with the mage temple of Cyþan. Perhaps, this is because they need constant re-enchantment.

Interestingly, the holy symbols of Cyþan are the bell and book used to keep the chant in tune, and remind the mages of what elements they are manipulating. At a large temple, some holy symbols may also be enchanted provide skill enhancements to mages. Secretly, some mages also use scrying crystals of an amber-like condensation found in the chimney and fire-pit of a forge.

Cups¶

As with rings, cups don’t have charges. The circular nature somehow permits the enchantment to resonate perpetually.

These are often created by craftspeople associated with the Temple of Folme. The enchantment if generally applied by mages from the temple of Cyþan. This makes them particularly expensive.

The holy symbol for the Temple of Folme are strings of beads, unrelated to cups they might make as a commission for someone wealthy and powerful. Holy beads from a temple may be enchanted to provide skill enhancements to the craftspeople who flock to the temple looking for work.

Swords¶

Swords are more like characters than they are like other items. This means they will gain some experience in parallel with the character wielding it. (The GM will apportion experience between the character and their sword.)

When a weapon or a piece of armor has enough Charisma, it will attempt to control the character. Each attempt by the weapon will be an opposed skill roll between the sword and the character. The attempt is up to the GM, and may occur at the worst possible time for the character.

Swords are the holy artifact for the temple of Hildeþrymm. This temple enforces strong restrictions on who is allowed to carry a sword. A specific oath is required to carry a sword to seek justice, offer mercy, and walk with humility.

Other weapons and armor can also be enchanted. Because simplicity is essential, this can require the deconstruction of complicated armor or weapon systems. For example, an enchanted bow-string is easier to create than an enchanted bow; a better string might provide for more damage. Instead of enchanted arrows (with wooden shafts, metal tips, and feather flights) a mage might place an enchantment on the flights; which could improve accuracy. An enchantment on the tips could improve armor penetration.

While it’s rarely important, enchanted swords may have charges. Because they are often used in life-and-death combat, an opponent’s injury will tend to recover a sword’s charges, making it rarely necessary to track the discharge of power. This is roughly parallel to the way a mage who can recover fatigue points through rest and meditation. The difference is that a sword recovers fatigue when it’s used to injure someone.

Armor¶

The shapes of armor make them difficult to enchant. The exceptions are crowns and round shields. A simple bronze or iron crown – without gems or other decorations – will provide some protection for the top of a wearer’s head. An enchanted crown can provide enhanced protection or other special abilities. A crown can be fit onto an appropriately sized-and-shaped helmet or metal cap; this creates a mixture of ordinary armor plus the enchantment of the crown.

Because of the shape, crowns or shields are not limited by charges.

Potions (Elixirs)¶

An elixir is – effectively – an enchantment in a bottle. It has a single-charge, and a short shelf-life between assembly and consumption. An elixir generally provides a Special Ability, enhanced Attribute, or even a specific Skill. Additional elixirs might remove a disadvantage temporarily.

A potion is not itself magical; this is unlike rings, wands, cups, etc., which are clearly enchanted. It provides an effect equivalent to an enchantment with additional aspects of one charge, a range of zero, and a duration of 5 minutes. They have an additional condition of a short shelf-life: at most 4D days.

Creating an elixir requires alchemical tools. If the ingredients are available, preparation generally takes one day. Two to four alchemists can reduce the time to a half a day or a quarter of a day. More than four alchemists will get in each other’s way. A roomful of alchemists is an accident waiting to happen.
