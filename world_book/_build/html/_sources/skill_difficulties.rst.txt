##############################
  Example Skill Difficulties
##############################

The "Character Basics" chapter provided brief descriptions of each
skill (and you will need to return to that chapter if you need a refresher).
When the generic difficulties aren't enough, look up various skills in
this chapter to get even more ideas on the challenge level for using
those skills in common situations.

|mod_opend6| See the :external:ref:`OpenD6 Fantasy, "Example Skill Difficulties"<fantasy.skill_difficulties>` chapter.
Some modifications will be required, however, for this world.

The **charioteering** skill is altered significantly to focus on controlling a horse pulling a drag sled.
This isn't difficult, and there's no real combat advantage to it, since drag sleds are slow, and a horse can't really gallop while pulling one.

The **devices** skill is difficult to separate from the **know-how** skill.
This world is generally limited to bronze-age devices and traps, which tend to be relatively simple.

The **lockpicking** skill, similarly, is essentially the same as the **know-how** skill,
since locks tend to be relatively primitive.

There are some new skills introduced by these rules.

Concealed Strike (Agility)
===========================

This is a specialized combat skill with two aspects.
First, it makes the character familiar with the unusual weapons of an assassin: things like a garrote, a leather sap, a knife hidden in a sleeve, a slender sword in a walking stick, a blade on the edge of a boot, etc.
Second, it gives the character specific combat moves to use an unusual weapon they've chosen.

With their preferred weapon, this skill's value (die plus pips) becomes an advantage in combat to ready and use that weapon in a single action on an **unsuspecting** opponent.
After a failure, there's no easy way to apply the weapon again, since the opponent has seen it and either blocked it, dodged it, or otherwise survived it.

While this doesn't require initiative (it might be used after a successful block, for example), it is aided by extra time in combat or an associated skill like stealth or concealment or bluff.

Poison Delivery (Coordination)
==============================

Handling poisons without getting accidentally injured can be tricky.
The deadly poisons need to be carefully contained and cautiously administered to avoid endangering the poisoner.
A successful skill roll administers the poison: this might be into food or drink, or onto a weapon.
It can even be ingested by the target if there's a successful *Charisma* or *persuasion* roll to convince them to eat or drink it.

A failure means the person handling the poison takes half the damage of the poison.
It also means the target may see through the attempt and defend themselves.

Note that the various poisons all have unique methods of administration, often require some setup time, and often have a expiration time once they've been applied to a weapon or an exposed surface.
In most cases, the setup time must be completed in full or the poison doesn't work. The expiration time is a "half-life": each expiration period cuts the effectiveness in half.

**Example**.
If a poison has an expiration of 5 minutes, then after 5 minutes, it drops to 1/2 effect.
After 10 minutes, it drops to 1/4 effect. In 15 minutes, it will slip 1/8 effect. Once the effect slips below 1D, there will be two remaining intervals where it does only 2 pips, and then 1 pip of damage, respectively.


The attempted administration can benefit from taking extra time, and making appropriate additional skill rolls for stealth, hiding, etc.
An Alchemy skill roll, for example, can make poison administration easier.

Alchemy (Acumen)
============================

At a general level, this is the essence of distilling, purifying, and combining things.
It includes a little microbiology to cultivate yeast for brewing and bread-baking.
At the more advanced levels, it involves poisons and elixirs.

It helps in the proper magical realm of creating magical effects through potions.

The difficulties are generally based on the quality of the ingredients, the character's understanding of the procedure, and the availability of the required tools and equipment.

The modifiers for crafting can be used here, also.

Social Graces (Charisma)
========================

In a violent, stratified society, proper social behavior is a form of self-defense.
Knowing a bit of who is important, and how they are properly addressed is common knowledge.
The isolation of strata means that many commoners rarely interact directly with nobility.
Servants, on the other hand, must constantly code-switch between commoners and their masters.

Each rank of social distance adds +1 to the difficulty of an interaction. And yes, the king can find the slang of criminals and outcasts utterly opaque, with a +9 difficulty.

For the most part, the social hierarchy works like this.

0. Outcasts, outlaws, criminals, beggars, etc.

1. Farmers, animal tenders, and crafts people. Conscripted fighters, and untrained guards, also.

2. Servants of the lesser nobility.

3. At-large and lay members of the temples. Squires, acolyte mages, priests in training, apprentice craftspeople, nursemaids and orderlies. Members at large of the secretive sects.

4. General members of the temples. Trained fighters, ring mages, priests, highly-skilled crafts-people, healers. Some secretive sects have priests at this rank, also.

5. Ranking members. Lesser knights, high mages, high priests, journeying craftsmen, journeying healers.

6. Great members. High knights, great mages of a temple's tower, great priest of a temple, master crafter, master healers.

7. Great(er) members. The High Knight serving an earl. The great mage of the network of temples, the great priest of a network of temples, the great crafter of a guild, the master healer of an earl.

8. Nobility. The families of the earls. The family of the king. A few trusted advisors.

9. Royalty: the earls, and the King.

Yes, there's a lot. The Great and Great(er) members of a temple are endlessly confusing.
Because it's difficult to know what's going on in remote parts of an earldom, or another earldom, knowing who -- precisely -- is the Great Priest of a temple can be a serious social problem.
This can lead to demands for satisfaction, duels, and trials by combat.
